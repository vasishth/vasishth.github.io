Current Issues in Sentence Comprehension (東京大学)

In this course, we will read some of the recent literature on retrieval and prediction processes in sentence comprehension. I will start by discussing some recent work on retrieval from my own lab. But most of the focus will be on predictive processes, because I think there some interesting open issues there, and because Japanese seems like a really good language to study the open problems in predictive parsing.
Prerequisities: Some background and interest in sentence comprehension research is desirable.
Syllabus and readings: The syllabus is provided here as a pdf. Readings are provided as a zip archive, password protected: download from here. I will give out the password in class.
Slides: To be provided.

Approximate schedule:
Please look at the reading list in the pdf for the references (the articles will be released as a zip archive to participants). The general style of the lectures will be that I will present the material in class, and the participants should feel free to interrupt and discuss the points that come up. Since everything in this work is highly dependent on statistical methods, I will occasionally make excursions into that area to illustrate a particular point. Also, I will discuss other papers that are not on the list, when this makes sense.
Active participation is strongly encouraged!

    The slides are here.

    Here are the ERP papers I mentioned.
  5. １１月１８日: In this lecture, I will talk about readings 4 and 5. The experiment involving Chinese relatives is interesting in many ways because the history of Chinese relative clause research is a truly bizarre and fantastic one, and worthy of study in its own right as a case study of psycholinguistics. The slides, which only cover the Linzen and Jaeger paper, are here.
  6. １１月２５日 In this lecture, I will talk about three papers (readings 6-8) on a topic is especially interesting for head-final languages: ungrammatical center embeddings. In English, these can be rather bizarre, but in German, triple center embeddings are not that uncommon in formal writing. We will discuss some of the explanations for why ungrammatical triple CEs are considered good in English (compared to their grammatical counterparts) and why grammatical ones are better in German (compared to their grammatical counterparts). Haeussler and Bader have an interesting alternative position on this that we will also discuss. The slides are here.
  7. １２月０２日 In this lecture we will shift gears and look at some work from the Maryland group on gap filling, and the timing of verb selection in Japanese sentence production (readings 9, 10). The first paper is about unexpected verb continuations after the pre-verbal structure raises some expectations about the upcoming verb (type), and the second paper addresses incrementality in production. I will also discuss the replication results of the Levy and Keller 2013 paper we discussed some time back.
    The slides for the replication of Levy and Keller 2013 are here. The slides discussing the assigned readings are here.
